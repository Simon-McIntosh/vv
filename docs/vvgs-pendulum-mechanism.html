ITER VVGS Gravitational Centring — Mechanism, Geometry & Confidence

Simon McIntosh  ·  2026-05-28  ·  companion to the main lateral-displacement report

Purpose. The main report concludes that the 9 inclined VVGS supports act as a stable gravitational pendulum in the lateral plane and self-centre the vessel at rest. This companion derives that result from first principles, draws how the effective pendulum length Leff is constructed geometrically, examines limit cases (vertical struts and α = 45°), and is honest about how confident we should be in the model and where the remaining holes are.

§1 — The single-strut geometry

Each VVGS strut is a near-vertical link whose axis is tilted α = 15° from vertical, leaning inward. Its ground anchor sits at radius Rs ≈ 10 m on a horizontal support ring under the lower ports. Extending the strut's central axis upward, it meets the vertical machine axis at a height

h(α) = Rs / tan(α)

above the support ring. For the operational 15°, h ≈ 37.3 m — far above the vessel. The vessel's centre of gravity sits roughly zCoG ≈ 5 m above the support attachment (this is an estimate — see §4 on confidence). The effective pendulum length is then

Leff(α) = h(α) − zCoG = Rs / tan(α) − zCoG.

§2 — From single struts to a stable pendulum

A single near-vertical strut with a load on top is, taken by itself, an inverted pendulum: ground pivot at the bottom, mass perched above — unstable. What converts the system into a stable pendulum is the symmetric arrangement of nine inclined struts: every strut's axis, extended upward, meets the machine axis at the same point P. Because all the support-axis lines pass through P, the rigid vessel can only execute small motions that look like a rotation about P. P is the system's virtual pivot, and Leff = h − zCoG is the distance from P to the centre of gravity.

Because the motion is a pendulum, the lateral restoring stiffness (the force per unit horizontal displacement of the CoG) is the standard result

K = W / Leff,        Tn = 2π · √(Leff / g),

with W = M g the supported weight (M ≈ 9000 t). For α = 15° this gives Leff ≈ 32.3 m, K ≈ 2.73 kN/mm and a natural period Tn ≈ 11.4 s — a soft pendulum because the geometry forces Leff to be long.

Limit case: vertical struts (α → 0°)

With perfectly vertical struts the strut-axis lines never meet — the convergence point P recedes to infinity. Leff → ∞ and K → 0. There is no gravitational centring force: a small lateral perturbation produces no restoring torque, and the vessel sits in a marginal/neutral equilibrium that is easily upset. Each individual strut, viewed in isolation, is now exactly an inverted pendulum; only the inward inclination converts the system into a stable pendulum by creating a virtual pivot above the CoG.

Limit case: α = 45°

At α = 45° the convergence point sits at h = Rs = 10 m above the support attachment. With zCoG ≈ 5 m the effective pendulum is only Leff ≈ 5.0 m — about an order of magnitude shorter than at 15°. The stiffness K = W/Leff rises by the same factor and the natural period drops to Tn ≈ 4.5 s. This is a much stiffer pendulum: a more strongly centred vessel, but a strut tilt that would be impractical for a gravity support carrying ~9000 t. The 15° design value is a deliberate balance between centring stiffness and structural practicality.

Critical inclination — the system becomes unstable

If α is increased far enough that h = zCoG, the convergence point P falls to the level of the CoG and Leff → 0; beyond this critical angle

αcrit = arctan(Rs / zCoG) ≈ 63°

P lies below the CoG. The vessel is then a true inverted pendulum about P and is unstable: any small lateral perturbation grows. The operational α = 15° sits far on the stable side of this transition with substantial margin.

§4 — Confidence & holes in the argument

How confident should we be that this mechanism actually works as described? The qualitative result — that the inclined-hinge VVGS provides a stable gravitational centring force — is robust. The quantitative numbers (Leff, K, Tn) carry moderate uncertainty from the parameters we have estimated rather than measured.

Where the argument can be tightened — known holes

  1. Estimated CoG height (zCoG). We took zCoG ≈ 5 m above the support ring. The actual VV + in-vessel CoG depends on the blanket + divertor distribution and is design-specific. Leff is sensitive to this (≈ 2 m per metre of zCoG shift); a 1–2 m uncertainty in zCoG propagates to a similar uncertainty in Leff and a few-percent shift in Tn.
  2. The "rigid pin-pin strut" idealisation. The actual VVGS is a dual-hinge mechanism — primary and secondary hinges with four dowels — not a simple pin-pin link. The convergence-point argument requires that the effective line of action of each support pass through a common point P, which holds for any kinematic chain that, taken as a whole, is rotation-only about a single axis. The dual-hinge geometry is designed precisely to give one rotational DOF (radial); confirming that the chain's effective line of action passes through a single P (and not, say, two intermediate instantaneous centres) needs the as-built hinge dimensions.
  3. The 15° reference. We have read "15° inclination" from the ITER literature as from vertical (a slightly tilted near-vertical strut). If the inclination is in fact stated from horizontal (a strut closer to horizontal than vertical), the geometry is very different. The "leaning inward" language and the centring rationale make the from-vertical reading by far the more natural one, and the literature's framing ("stable equilibrium") only makes sense in that case — but the precise angular reference should be confirmed against the engineering drawing.
  4. Supported mass M. We use M ≈ 9000 t for VV + in-vessel components (ITER VV Load Spec + mass table, ITER_D_6TLUDY). The VV alone is ~5200 t; the full in-vessel set brings it to the ~9 kt range. K = W/Leff scales with M, so K could be 15–20 % softer if a smaller effective M (say only the VV) actually moves with the lateral mode.
  5. Other elastic stiffness contributions. The pendulum analysis is purely gravitational. The supports also have elastic compliance (the dowel material, the bracket, the linkage), which contributes additional lateral stiffness in parallel. Our centring estimate is therefore a lower bound on the actual lateral stiffness — the system is at least as stiff as the gravitational pendulum, possibly stiffer once the elastic terms are added.
  6. Symmetry. The virtual-pivot argument requires the 9 struts to be nominally symmetric. Installation tolerances will break that symmetry at the mm-scale; in the small-perturbation, low-frequency lateral mode this is a higher-order effect and does not move the qualitative picture.

§5 — External evidence

The model is anchored in the published ITER VVGS design literature; we are not relying on the convergence-point pendulum as a private deduction.

  • The 15° inclination and its centring function are explicit design intent. The structural-design literature on the ITER VVGS states (verbatim): "Inclination of 15° for the hinge based supporting system was introduced to provide a centering force to keep a stable equilibrium state of the vacuum vessel. Due to this inclination the hinges are rotated by the radial expansion of the VV" (Fusion Eng. Des., 2018 — structural integrity analysis for the manufacturing design of the ITER VVGS). This confirms both the inclination value and the qualitative claim made here.
  • The dual-hinge / dowel mechanism is documented in the same literature ("Each VVGS consists of one primary hinge, one secondary hinge, upper and lower blocks and 4 dowels … radial movement is permitted by the rotation of dowels, while toroidal and vertical movement is restricted by the rigidity of the dowels and that of the primary hinge").
  • The "soft landing" of VV sector #6 onto its gravity support is described in the ITER news article on the first VVGS contact; this confirms the operational geometry and the assembly-tolerance framing used in §1 of the main report (the 3 mm margin quoted in the news article matches the toroidal slot used here).
  • Independent mechanical-testing literature on the VV support structure (coating screening, multi-axial mock-up tests, lubricant coating of the dowel) confirms that the design carries the loads as a dual-hinge / dowel system rather than as a simple solid strut.
Net. The gravitational centring mechanism is a textbook inclined-strut / virtual-pivot result, applied to a configuration that ITER has deliberately designed for centring (per the published structural-analysis literature on the VVGS). The qualitative claim — vessel self-centres in a stable pendulum well with a long Leff and a soft natural frequency — is on firm ground. The numerical values (Leff ≈ 32 m, K ≈ 2.7 kN/mm, Tn ≈ 11 s) are accurate to roughly ±20 % given the estimated zCoG and supported mass; pinning them more tightly needs the as-built hinge geometry and the lateral mass that actually moves with the n=1 mode.
