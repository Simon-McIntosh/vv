ITER Vacuum Vessel — Lateral Displacement Analysis

Simon McIntosh  ·  2026-05-28

5,000-sample Monte Carlo  ·  scipy HiGHS LP  ·  3-DOF rigid-body model  ·  metric: max lateral displacement of the VV centre from the gravitational centre (n = 1 first-wall shift)

Summary of findings. The vessel rests on nine inward-inclined (15°) gravity supports that form a soft lateral gravitational pendulum (K ≈ 2.7 kN/mm, Tn ≈ 11 s). Whether the vessel actually self-centres is decided by friction, and that selects between two bounding pictures:
  • Frictionless lower bound — self-centring (§3–§6). If the supports slid freely, the vessel would return to the gravitational centre at rest (≈ 0 contribution to the n = 1 first-wall shift) and only forced excursions would reach the departure-from-centre envelope — nominal 1.55 mm, distribution-dependent tail to ~3 mm.
  • Realistic bound — stiction walk (§10). The dead-weight axial load per VVGS is ≈ 10 MN; even with low-friction hinges the toroidal breakaway force is ≈ 1 MN per support (≈ 5.7 MN to translate the whole vessel) — roughly 1000× the ≈ 4 kN gravitational restoring force. Gravity therefore cannot recentre the vessel: once a large (disruption / VDE) load slides it, it stays, and over many events it ratchets within the kinematic polytope. The relevant n = 1 envelope is then the polytope width ≈ 3.1 mm (the nominal ceiling, §9) — about half the 6 mm budget.
  • Thermal cycling does not recentre it (§11). Warming to 100 °C grows the vessel radially ~12 mm (freely accommodated by dowel rotation), but the off-centre toroidal demand at the slots is only ~µm — orders of magnitude too small to break the MN-scale stiction. The vessel keeps whatever position it walked to.
  • The statistics are only as good as the gap distribution (§12). The assumed Uniform(±1.5 mm) prior is not validated, so the Monte-Carlo percentiles are illustrative only. The robust results are the distribution-free kinematic bounds and the force comparison above. Measuring the nine gaps with feeler gauges collapses the problem to a deterministic calculation and is less effort than justifying any input distribution — it is the recommended path.

§1 — Problem & Assumptions

The vacuum vessel rests on nine gravity supports (VVGS) equally spaced toroidally beneath the lower ports, on a ring of radius Rs ≈ 10 m. Each VVGS is a dual-hinge mechanism inclined at 15° from vertical, leaning inward toward the machine axis. Radial motion of the vessel is permitted by rotation of the dowels (this accommodates the thermal expansion between assembly and operation/baking); toroidal and vertical motion are restrained by the dowels' rigidity. The toroidal restraint is a ±1.5 mm (3 mm total) dowel slot.

Assembly assumption — the source of mobility

At each VVGS the connecting pin is oriented in the toroidal direction and runs through interleaved vessel-side and ground-side tabs. The holes are tight on the pin (no radial play); the toroidal clearance is the spacing between adjacent tabs along the pin. That inter-tab spacing is required to bring the parts together so the tabs can be aligned and the pin can be inserted — without it the support cannot be assembled. The same spacing then permits the vessel-side tabs to slide along the pin (toroidally) once assembled, giving ±1.5 mm (3 mm total) of lateral mobility per support. If the joints are left unshimmed — the case treated throughout this report — this assembly clearance is what drives the analysis.

For the Monte-Carlo sections (§5–§6) we adopt the prior that each support's pin offset ui is independent across the 9 supports and uniformly distributed on [−1.5 mm, +1.5 mm]. This prior is not validated — neither the uniform shape nor the ±1.5 mm range is established (§12) — so the resulting percentiles are illustrative, and the load-bearing conclusions of this report are deliberately the distribution-free ones (the kinematic bounds and the force comparison). Shimming a support fixes its ui to a measured value and removes that support's contribution to the mobility (§6).

Gravitational centring

The inward 15° inclination is deliberate. The nine support axes converge above the vessel centre of gravity; for the lateral (n = 1) mode the vessel therefore behaves as if suspended from that convergence point — an inclined-hinge gravitational pendulum — and the lateral plane has a parabolic potential well centred on the nominal position. The effective pendulum length is Leff ≈ Rs/tan(15°) − zCoG ≈ 32 m (≈ 26–32 m over the estimated zCoG = 5–10 m range). The lateral restoring stiffness is K = W/Leff. A companion note — "VVGS Gravitational Centring — Mechanism, Geometry & Confidence" — derives this from first principles with annotated diagrams and a side-on rocking animation of the 4-bar mechanism, examines the α → 0° and α = 45° limits, and assesses the holes and external evidence for the model.

Consequence — in the frictionless idealisation. With no friction, and no applied lateral force, the vessel would sit at the gravitational centre regardless of the assembly offsets — the pins simply occupy their ui positions in the slots and the vessel is on-axis. This is the lower-bound picture of §3–§6. §10 shows that dead-weight friction at the supports is ~1000× the centring force, so in reality the vessel does not return to centre once displaced — it holds its last position. Both bounds are carried through this report.

§2 — Constraint Model, Polytope & Linear Programming

We model the vacuum vessel as a rigid body with three in-plane degrees of freedom — two horizontal translations Δx, Δy and a rotation Δθ about the vertical axis (collected as q = [Δx, Δy, Δθ]). Each of the nine supports imposes a single linear inequality constraint: the toroidal slide of the vessel relative to its dowel must stay within the ±1.5 mm slot. For support i at toroidal angle φi = π/2 − 2πi/9 on the support ring at radius R = 8 m, the slide is

δᵢ = −sin(φᵢ)·Δx + cos(φᵢ)·Δy + R·Δθ  ≡  A[i, :] · q ,
   |uᵢ + δᵢ| ≤ 1.5 mm    for all i = 1 … 9,

so the displacement is bounded by 18 linear inequalities (one upper and one lower bound per support). The 9×3 constraint matrix has AᵀA = diag(4.5, 4.5, 576) — exactly diagonal, so the three rigid-body DOFs are mutually uncorrelated.

Polytope. The set of vessel positions that simultaneously satisfy all 18 inequalities is a convex region of (Δx, Δy, Δθ) space bounded by flat faces — geometrically a polytope (the three-dimensional generalisation of a polygon). Projected onto the horizontal plane (Δx, Δy), with Δθ optimised at each direction, it is a closed convex polygon of feasible lateral VV-centre positions — the displacement polytope. Its shape depends on the assembly offsets u and its diameter is set by the ±1.5 mm slot.

Linear program (LP). A linear program optimises a linear objective subject to linear inequality constraints — the canonical method for problems of exactly this kind. We solve, in each direction θ, the LP that maximises the projection of q on the unit vector (cos θ, sin θ, 0) subject to the 18 inequalities above; the optimum lies at the polytope vertex furthest along that direction. Sweeping θ traces the polytope boundary, and the maximum of ||q[:2]|| over the sweep is our metric: the maximum lateral displacement of the VV centre from the gravitational centre that any applied force can drive the vessel to. We use the HiGHS solver (scipy.optimize.linprog); each LP solves in milliseconds.

§7 — Start-Up Lateral Loads: Passive Self-Alignment

During plasma current ramp-up (0 → ~3 MA over ~10 s), the time-changing plasma current induces image currents in the vessel. These currents interact with the toroidal-field asymmetry produced by TF vault closure tolerances (a long-wavelength n ≤ 4 perturbation) to produce a lateral n = 1 force on the vessel.

Important: the start-up displacement direction is favourable. The lateral force on the induced vessel current is such that the vessel is pulled toward the n = 1 magnetic axis of the (offset) toroidal field — passive vessel currents act to self-align the wall with the field profile. Any vessel motion during the ramp therefore reduces the peak-to-peak misalignment between the n ≤ 4 first-wall and toroidal-field profiles; it consumes none of the 6 mm budget, and in fact frees some of it back. The start-up ramp is a budget-relieving event for the n = 1 first-wall criterion, not a budget-eroding one.

Force scale & timescale

Order-of-magnitude: with vessel image current of O(105 A) during the ramp, BT ≈ 5 T, fractional n = 1 field error ε ~ 10−4–10−3, and a toroidal length scale ~ 2πR, the lateral force Framp ~ ε · Ivv · BT · L is of order 1–10 kN. This is comparable to the gravitational centring threshold Fstop ≈ 4.1 kN but is far below the ≈ 1 MN per-support toroidal breakaway force (§10): a kN-scale start-up force does not slide the supports at all. The ramp duration (10 s) is comparable to the pendulum natural period (Tn ≈ 11 s), so in the frictionless idealisation the quasi-static deflection F/K = 0.4–4 mm would appear as a peak transient excursion of order 1–4 mm in the favourable (self-aligning) direction. In the realistic (stiction-dominated) picture the start-up force is two-to-three orders of magnitude below breakaway, so it produces no net lateral motion — it neither erodes nor relieves the budget kinematically. Either way this source is not budget-limiting; a detailed EM transient is required to pin the precise value.

§8 — Disruption Loads & VVGS Impact Case

Disruptions and asymmetric vertical displacement events (VDEs) deliver the large lateral loads. The bounding magnitudes and durations are defined in the ITER VV Load Specification (VVLS) and are substantially larger than the illustrative impulse used in an earlier draft of this note; the values below are order-of-magnitude only and should be taken from the VVLS for design.

Correction. An earlier version quoted a lateral impulse J ~ 10⁵ N·s over ~10 ms (free-mass velocity ~12 mm/s). Both the magnitude and the duration are lower than the VVLS bounding values, so the resulting toroidal VVGS force was correspondingly under-stated. The VVLS net horizontal VV loads are of order MN to tens of MN.

Two regimes follow from the friction analysis (§10), with breakaway ≈ 5.7 MN aggregate (≈ 1 MN per support):

  • Small / illustrative impulse (below breakaway). The earlier J ~ 10⁵ N·s carries kinetic energy ½Mv² ≈ 0.56 kJ (M ≈ 9000 t). The friction work over a full 3 mm slide is Fbrk·Δ ≈ 5.7 MN × 3 mm ≈ 17 kJ ≫ 0.56 kJ, so such an impulse would move the vessel only ~0.1 mm before stiction arrests it — there is no free-flight impact on the stops, contrary to the earlier free-mass picture.
  • VVLS bounding loads (above breakaway). Net horizontal loads of MN-to-tens-of-MN exceed the ≈ 5.7 MN breakaway, so the vessel does slide during major events. Each event displaces it by an amount set by the load, the impulse and the remaining slot; the displacement is retained (gravity cannot pull it back, §10). Repeated events make the vessel ratchet within the polytope (§9–§10).
The disruption/VDE toroidal force is the bounding VVGS lateral load case — and it is the mechanism that drives the vessel away from the gravitational centre toward the polytope-width bound. Its magnitude must be read from the VVLS; the analysis here establishes the consequence (irreversible walk), not the load value.

§9 — Maximum Rattle: the Polytope-Width Bound

§3–§6 measured the departure from the gravitational centre — the right metric if the vessel self-centres. This section measures the complementary quantity: the full peak-to-peak width of the displacement polytope (its diameter along the worst direction), computed with the same HiGHS LP (vv_viz.max_rattle_mm). The width is the total lateral travel available to the vessel — the kinematic ceiling on how far it can walk if friction (not gravity) sets its rest position (§10).

Width and departure behave oppositely. Departure-from-centre is minimised at the centred assembly (u = 0 → 1.55 mm) and grows for offset assemblies. The polytope width is maximised at the centred assembly and shrinks for offset assemblies, because a generic offset cuts the feasible set asymmetrically. The worst-case width is therefore the nominal one.

Nominal polytope width = 3.09 mm (distribution-free ceiling). Over the (illustrative) assembly population: median 2.24 mm, P5 1.19 mm, minimum 0.21 mm — a typical as-built assembly has a narrower reachable envelope than nominal.

§10 — Which Bound? Frictionless Self-Centring vs Stiction Walk

The two metrics correspond to two physical regimes. Which one ITER occupies is decided by a single comparison: the gravitational restoring force that would recentre the vessel, versus the friction (stiction) force that resists toroidal sliding at the supports.

Stiction wins by ~1000×. The gravitational restoring force (kN) is utterly negligible against the toroidal breakaway force (MN). The vessel does not self-centre. Once any load large enough to break stiction (a disruption/VDE, §8) slides the supports, the vessel moves and stays there — gravity cannot pull it back. Over many such events the vessel ratchets / walks through the polytope, biased only weakly by gravity and by any systematic load direction. The frictionless self-centring picture of §3–§6 is therefore a lower bound; the realistic operational envelope is the polytope width (§9), ≈ 3.1 mm at worst.

The mechanism — a soft gravitational pendulum that nonetheless cannot overcome dry friction — is the same one drawn dynamically in the companion note's side-on rocking animation of the 15° dual-hinge 4-bar supports: the restoring torque exists, but it is ~1000× too weak to move the supports against their dead-weight-driven stiction.

Where on the walk is any given machine? The vessel does not necessarily reach the full width — that depends on the number and size of breakaway-exceeding events and their directions over the machine life. The polytope width is the kinematic ceiling; the actual excursion is bounded by it and is, again, best resolved by measuring the gaps (§12) rather than modelled from an assumed load history.

§11 — Thermal Cycling & the Statically-Overdetermined Structure

Does warming the vessel (assembly ~22 °C → baking) relax the nine stictions in a coordinated way that lets it settle back to the gravitational centre? And what loads does an off-centre vessel generate when it is heated, given that nine supports over-constrain a body with only three in-plane rigid-body freedoms?

Free radial breathing — large, but it does not recentre

For ΔT ≈ 78 K (22 → 100 °C) and 316L(N) α ≈ 16×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, the support radius grows by Rs·α·ΔT ≈ 12.5 mm (≈ 32 mm at the full 200 °C bake). This is exactly the motion the dual-hinge supports are designed to accommodate: the dowels rotate and the radial growth is taken freely, generating no toroidal slot demand and no lateral load — provided the expansion is about the support-ring centre.

Thermal cycling does not break the stiction. If the vessel is off-centre by d, isotropic expansion about its own centroid produces a toroidal slot demand at each support of δith = α·ΔT·(d·êt,i) — magnitude only α·ΔT·|d| ≈ 3.7 µm for d = 3 mm. The elastic force to absorb such a micron-scale mismatch at a locked slot is of order a few kN — ~1000× below the ≈ 1 MN stiction. Heating therefore neither unloads the supports (the weight, and hence the normal load and stiction, are temperature-independent) nor generates enough toroidal demand to slide them. An off-centre vessel stays off-centre through thermal cycles. There is no coordinated relaxation back to the gravitational centre.

Locked-in loads: uniform expansion is benign; asymmetry is the risk

The key insight for the over-determined structure: uniform isotropic expansion of an off-centre vessel maps exactly onto a representable rigid-body shift, because δith = α·ΔT·(d·êt,i) = A[i,:]·(α·ΔT·dx, α·ΔT·dy, 0). A demand that lies in the column space of the constraint matrix A is a rigid-body mode — it induces no self-stress (only the µm-scale elastic take-up above if a slot is locked). So uniform heating of an over-constrained but nominally symmetric vessel is benign.

The genuine thermal risk is support-to-support asymmetry: non-uniform temperatures (sector-to-sector gradients, asymmetric baking, local heating) produce a toroidal demand pattern that does not lie in the column space of A. That component cannot be relieved by any rigid-body motion, so in a statically over-determined ring it must be carried as a self-equilibrating internal load set distributed across the redundant supports — locked-in toroidal forces at the dowels that scale with the differential expansion (α·ΔT·ΔRsupport-to-support), not with the mean temperature. A few tens of µm of differential growth against the MN-stiff, stiction-locked slots can build support loads far larger than the gravitational scale.

Conjecture (needs FE confirmation). The dominant thermal lateral concern is not mean-temperature recentring (it does not happen) but the locked-in self-stress from asymmetric thermal fields acting on a stiction-locked, over-determined 9-support ring. A thermo-mechanical FE model with realistic sector temperature spreads and the measured/assumed slot states is required to size these internal loads and confirm they remain within the VVGS toroidal capacity.

§12 — Reliability of the Gap Distribution & the Case for Measurement

The statistical results in §5–§6 rest on one weak assumption: that each support's as-built offset is an independent draw from Uniform(−1.5 mm, +1.5 mm). Neither part of that prior is justified. The real gaps are the product of an assembly sequence — sector welds, fit-up, shimming decisions, gravity sag — not a random process, and there is no evidence that the range is ±1.5 mm or that the shape is uniform. A different but equally defensible prior moves the percentiles materially, so the P95/P99 numbers carry little weight.

Recommendation: measure, don't propagate. Because the conclusions that matter are distribution-free, and the only distribution-dependent outputs are the unreliable percentiles, the highest-value action is to measure the nine VVGS gaps directly with feeler gauges. Nine measurements turn the problem into a deterministic kinematic calculation — the exact polytope, its width, and the as-built offset of the vessel centre — with no prior to defend. As the reviewer notes, this is also less effort than constructing and justifying an input-uncertainty model. Each measured gap also tightens the conditional bound (§6); nine collapse it entirely.

§13 — The 6 mm n ≤ 4 First-Wall Budget

The start-up heat-load criterion limits the peak-to-peak difference between the n ≤ 4 filtered first-wall profile and the n ≤ 4 filtered toroidal-field profile to ≤ 6 mm. The VV n = 1 lateral displacement is one contributor to the first-wall side of this inequality — and the two bounds (§10) give two very different answers:

PictureVV n = 1 contribution to the 6 mm budget
Frictionless self-centring — at rest, between shots≈ 0 mm
Start-up ramp (passive currents → wall follows TF)net reduces mismatch (kN force, no slide)
From-centre kinematic worst (forced, frictionless)≈ 2.98 mm
Stiction walk — realistic worst case (polytope width, §9)≈ 3.1 mm
The realistic VV n = 1 contribution is up to ≈ 3 mm — about half the 6 mm budget — not ≈ 0. Because stiction prevents self-centring (§10), the vessel sits wherever it was last driven, anywhere within the ±-width polytope, until the gaps are measured or the supports are shimmed. The optimistic ≈ 0 mm (self-centred) value should not be assumed for the budget. The remaining ~3 mm is what is then available for the toroidal-field side (mainly TF vault closure n ≤ 4) and other first-wall contributors — a materially tighter allocation than the self-centred picture implies.
How to recover budget. Measuring the nine gaps (§12) both removes the distribution uncertainty and quantifies the as-built offset; shimming a support fixes its ui and shrinks the reachable polytope (§6), pulling the realistic contribution back down toward the nominal envelope. These are deterministic, low-effort levers — far more reliable than the unvalidated statistical bound.

§ Followups

FE thermal-asymmetry loads, VVLS disruption loads, and a gap-measurement campaign

The analysis establishes that the vessel walks (does not self-centre) and that thermal cycling does not recentre it. Three open items remain to harden the conclusions for the engineering review: (1) the locked-in self-stress from asymmetric thermal fields on the overdetermined ring needs an FE model; (2) the bounding disruption/VDE toroidal VVGS force should be read from the VV Load Specification (VVLS) rather than the order-of-magnitude estimate; (3) the unvalidated gap prior should be replaced by direct feeler-gauge measurement of the 9 gaps.
