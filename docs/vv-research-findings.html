ITER VV Lateral Rattle — Research Findings

Revised 2026-05-28  ·  5,000-sample MC  ·  scipy HiGHS LP  ·  3-DOF rigid-body model  ·  metric: max departure from gravitational centre (n=1 first-wall shift)

Key result: The VV is supported by 9 inward-inclined dual-hinge gravity supports that act as a gravitational pendulum well in the lateral DOF — the vessel self-centres at rest. The relevant rattle metric is therefore the maximum forced departure from the gravitational centre, not the peak-to-peak range. With the toroidal slot at ±1.5 mm (confirmed), the nominal LP envelope half-width is 1.547 mm; the 5000-sample MC over independent assembly offsets gives P95 = 2.27 mm, P99 = 2.53 mm, median 1.53 mm, mode ≈ 1.49 mm, max observed 2.98 mm — single-sided departures from centre under sufficient forced load. Two EM load cases drive forced departure: (a) the 0→3 MA, 10 s plasma current ramp (estimated lateral force comparable to the gravitational restoring; near-resonant with the ~10 s natural period but single-event, so peak excursions stay O(1 mm)); and (b) disruptions (MN-scale impulse → vessel velocity → impact at the ±1.5 mm stops — a potentially bounding dynamic load case for the VVGS).

§1 — Physical Mechanism: the Inclined-Hinge Gravity Support

The ITER Vacuum Vessel rests on 9 gravity supports (VVGS) equally spaced toroidally beneath the lower ports, on a ring of radius Rs ≈ 8 m. Each VVGS is a dual-hinge mechanism inclined at 15° from vertical, leaning inward toward the machine axis. The dual-hinge geometry permits radial motion of the vessel (needed for thermal expansion between assembly and the 100 °C operation / 200 °C bake conditions) by rotation of the dowels, while the dowels' rigidity restrains toroidal and vertical motion. The toroidal restraint takes the form of a ±1.5 mm (3 mm total) dowel slot — confirmed against the GS design.

The inclination is deliberate — it provides a gravitational centering force. The 15° inward tilt arranges the nine support struts so that their axes converge on the machine vertical axis at a point above the vessel centre of gravity. For the lateral (n = 1) mode the vessel therefore behaves as if suspended from that convergence point — an inclined-hinge gravitational pendulum. The radial DOF is not free; it sits in a parabolic potential well centred on the nominal position.

Centering-force model

With the convergence point at height h = Rs/tan(15°) ≈ 31.3 m above the support attachments and the VV CoG roughly 5 m above the supports, the effective pendulum length is Leff ≈ 26 m. (Leff is sensitive to the inclination — about 2 m per degree of angle change.) The lateral restoring stiffness is

Klateral  =  W / Leff

where W is the supported weight. With M ≈ 8000 t (VV plus in-vessel components) → W ≈ 78 MN, the headline parameters are:

§2 — How the Centering Force Reframes the Analysis

The centering force changes the nature of the problem. The earlier kinematic model (vessel free to sit anywhere within the ±1.5 mm gaps) gave a peak-to-peak envelope of 3.09 mm and treated the assembly offsets ui as setting the vessel's rest position. With the gravitational well, the picture changes:

  • At rest the vessel self-centres to the gravitational minimum (q = 0, n = 1 ≈ 0), regardless of the toroidal assembly offsets. The pins simply sit at ui in their ±1.5 mm slots; the vessel is centred. Between shots the gravitational restoring re-centres the wall.
  • The relevant metric becomes the maximum forced departure from centre (single-sided), not the peak-to-peak range. The departure is bounded by the kinematic gap polytope, but only reached when a lateral force exceeds the soft restoring.
  • The polytope itself is the kinematic ceiling on forced departure — the furthest the VV centre can be pushed from q = 0 under any external force. It depends on the assembly offsets but has a hard nominal envelope half-width of 1.547 mm.
  • Disruption impacts at the stops become a dynamic load case: a MN-scale EM impulse over ~ms accelerates the vessel; the soft centering barely decelerates it; it hits the dowel stops with kinetic energy that the VVGS must absorb.

§3 — Constraint Model & LP

For a rigid-body displacement q = [Δx, Δy, Δθ], the toroidal slide at support i is

δᵢ = −sin(φᵢ)·Δx + cos(φᵢ)·Δy + R·Δθ     (= A[i,:]·q)

with φᵢ = π/2 − 2πi/9 and R = 8 m. AᵀA = diag(4.5, 4.5, 576) — exactly diagonal, so the three DOFs are mutually orthogonal. The toroidal-slot constraint at each support is |ui + δᵢ| ≤ 1.5 mm. The set of reachable VV-centre positions from q = 0 is the convex projection of this 3-DOF polytope onto (Δx, Δy) — the rattle polytope shown in the diagrams. The maximum one-sided departure from q = 0 is the support function of this polytope, and is what we Monte Carlo over.

§4 — Why the Nominal Envelope Slightly Exceeds 1.5 mm

A single bracket travels ±1.5 mm yet the nominal envelope reaches 1.547 mm (and the peak-to-peak diameter 3.093 mm). The Δx coefficient at support i is −sin φᵢ, so the +X grip scales with |sin φᵢ|: tightest at top/bottom (φ = ±90°, |sin| = 1) and falls to zero at the sides (φ = 0/180°, |sin| = 0 — +X is purely radial there and free). The extra 0.047 mm beyond a single bracket's 1.5 mm comes from the rotation DOF — the LP finds a 3-support binding vertex (supports 0, 4, 5) at which Δθ = +5.83 µrad allows Δx = 1.547 mm. This is the (small) geometric reason the polytope is slightly larger than a single bracket slot.

§8 — Effect of Gap Measurements (much more powerful under this metric)

This is an important reversal from the previous (peak-to-peak diameter) framing. The polytope diameter is set by the 3 mm slot and is essentially insensitive to the assembly offsets — measuring gaps barely tightens it. The departure from the gravitational centre is governed by how far the polytope is offset from q = 0, and that offset is exactly what the ui set — so each measured gap directly tightens the conditional departure distribution. In the limit of all 9 measured and found near-centred, the conditional max departure collapses to the 1.55 mm nominal envelope; a typical-random measurement set narrows toward the unconditional distribution.

§9 — Rotation (n = 0, Separate Mode)

The LP for maximum Δθ gives a range of ±187.5 µrad (0.375 mrad): in pure rotation all 9 brackets slide synchronously by R·Δθ = ±1.5 mm and bind together. At ×1500 this is ±16°, very visible.

Rotation does not drive the n = 1 heat-load concern. Rotating an axisymmetric torus about its own axis leaves the plasma–wall gap unchanged (n = 0). The ±187.5 µrad mode shifts discrete features (tiles, ports) by ±1.5 mm at R = 8 m — a local-alignment consideration for ports/penetrations, not part of the n = 1 budget. The translation LP optimum uses only 5.83 µrad of rotation (invisible).

§10 — EM Implications

The lateral rattle is exercised by two distinct EM load cases. Both are now framed against the centering force and the kinematic departure ceiling.

§10.1 — Plasma current ramp (start-up)

The plasma is ramped from 0 to ~3 MA over ~10 s. dIp/dt drives image currents in the vessel; these currents in the presence of n ≤ 4 toroidal-field asymmetries (mainly from TF vault closure tolerances) produce a lateral n = 1 force on the vessel.

Force estimate (order of magnitude): with vessel image current of O(10⁵ A) during the ramp, BT ≈ 5 T, n = 1 field error ε ~ 10⁻⁴–10⁻³, and a vessel toroidal length scale ~ 2πR, the lateral force is Framp ~ ε · Ivv · BT · L ~ 1–10 kN — i.e. comparable to the Fstop ≈ 4.5 kN centering threshold. Detailed EM analysis is required for a defensible number.
Timescales — the ramp drives the vessel near its natural period. The 10 s current ramp ≈ the 10 s pendulum period (Tn). Quasi-static deflection xqs = Framp/K = 0.3–3 mm; with τ/Tn ≈ 1 the dynamic amplification factor for a single ramp is ~1.3–1.5 (it is not resonant accumulation — that requires periodic forcing — but it is enough to overshoot the static solution by ~50%). The estimated peak start-up departure is therefore O(1–4 mm) — potentially comparable to the ±1.5 mm gap. Whether the vessel reaches the dowel stops during a normal current ramp depends sensitively on the actual n = 1 force amplitude and on damping; this should be confirmed with the EM transient model.

§10.2 — Disruption / VDE

A current-quench EM impulse delivers a MN-scale lateral force over milliseconds, far faster than the 10 s vessel period and far larger than the kN-scale centering. On the disruption timescale the vessel responds essentially as a free mass:

impulse  J = ∫F dt   ~  (10 MN) · (10 ms)        ≈  10⁵ N·s
velocity v = J / M  ~  10⁵ / 8×10⁶ kg            ≈  12  mm/s
amplitude (free)  A = v / ω  ~  v · Tn / 2π   ≈  20  mm     (≫ 1.5 mm gap!)
The vessel hits the dowel stops with O(10 mm/s) closing velocity, carrying O(500 J) of kinetic energy. Peak force at impact depends on the stop's elastic compliance: for a relatively rigid stop (Kstop ~ GN/m), Fpeak = √(2 K E) is on the order of ~1 MN. This is an impulsive lateral load on the VVGS — distinct from steady-state gravity loading and potentially a bounding load case for VVGS lateral design. The soft centering provides no material deceleration before impact. Dynamic amplification through the linkage should be assessed.

§10.3 — The 6 mm n ≤ 4 budget

The start-up heat-load criterion compares the filtered (n ≤ 4) first-wall and toroidal-field profiles: their peak-to-peak difference must remain within 6 mm. The vessel's n = 1 contribution to the first-wall side of this inequality is precisely the departure-from-centre quantified above:
